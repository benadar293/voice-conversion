Adapting a Diffusion-based Music Synthesis Model to Human Voice Conversion

Ben Maman1, Frank Zalkow2, Hans-Ulrich Berendes1, Paolo Sani2, Christian Dittmar2, Meinard MÃ¼ller1,
1International Audio Laboratories Erlangen, Germany
2Fraunhofer IIS, Erlangen, Germany
Preprint

Abstract

Recent generative models have shown promising results in audio generation across various domains, including human speech, singing voice, and multi-instrument music synthesis. Such acoustic models are typically specialized, with separate systems for speech, singing, and instrumental music. However, real-world audio often comprises multiple domains—for instance, musical recordings that combine a sung melody or spoken lyrics with instrumental accompaniment. This highlights the need for more general-purpose approaches to audio synthesis that can handle such integration. As an initial step towards universal synthesis, in this work we compare different acoustic models originating from distinct domains—instrumental music synthesis and speech synthesis—on the task of human voice conversion. Through an extensive evaluation across singing and speech, we demonstrate that a diffusion-based instrumental music synthesis model can be effectively adapted to human voice conversion, achieving performance comparable to or surpassing that of a dedicated speech synthesis model. To facilitate training on large-scale, minimally curated datasets, we demonstrate that off-the-shelf feature extractors for phonetics, pitch and acoustics provide effective conditioning signals for the synthesizer, enabling self-supervised training.

Speech Voice Conversion Samples    [Go to top]

The samples in this section are from our proposed attention-based T5-Voc model trained on vocal data (speech and singing). This model was used originally for instrumental music synthesis. As can be seen, it can effectively be adapted to human voice conversion. The first column ("Source") provides real source excerpts which are converted across speakers. Each of the next columns corresponds to a target speaker. The first row provides a real reference excerpt for each target speaker ("Target").
Importantly, the version conditioning mechanism based on FiLM layers originally used to condition on acoustics, can be effectively applied to condition on the speaker.
For a comparison with the FlowMAC model based on MatchTTS and ForwardTacotron, see the section on the speech MUSHRA-like listening test.

Singing Voice Conversion Samples—Singstyle111 Dataset    [Go to top]

The samples in this section are from our proposed attention-based T5-Voc model trained on vocal data (speech and singing). Similarly to speech, this model originally used for instrumental music synthesis can be effectively adapted to singing voice conversion, with the FiLM-based version-conditioning mechanism applied to condition on the singer. The first column ("Source") provides real source excerpts which are converted across singers. Each of the next columns corresponds to a target singer. The first row provides a real reference excerpt for each target singer ("Target").
For a comparison with the FlowMAC model based on MatchTTS and ForwardTacotron, see the section on the singing MUSHRA-like listening test.
Most importantly, it can be observed that although the off-the-shelf phonetic posteriorgram extractor used to condition on phonetic content was trained on speech data only, it can still capture phonetic content in singing samples. The extracted phonetic content is then preserved when swapping the singer.

Speech MUSHRA-like Listening Test    [Go to top]

The samples in this section are from the speech MUSHRA-like listening test. The PAD-VC model is based on ForwardTacotron with a reconstruction loss. The MAC-Voc model is the FlowMAC model which is trained with a flow-matching objective, conditioned on the output of PAD-VC. T5-Voc is the T5-based diffusion model adapted from instrumental music synthesis. T5-All is the same model, trained on a dataset also containing instrumental music and vocal-instrumental mixtures.

Vocal–Instrumental Conversion Samples    [Go to top]

The samples in this section are from the attention-based T5-All model trained on vocal and instrumental data. Each column corresponds to a performer (singer or band). Each row corresponds to a music excerpt that is converted across performers. The instrumental music content was obtained through an automatically-transcribed piano roll. The piano roll does not include drums, therefore drums are generated implicitly according to the performer (for example, performances of Dietrich Fischer-Dieskau do not contain drums).
For vocal-only samples generated by this model, see sections on MUSHRA-like listening tests for speech and singing. Although in the paper we only evaluate vocal synthesis, these samples qualitatively demonstrate the potential of our approach to synthesize complex vocal-instrumental mixtures, the investigation of which remains future work.

Feature Extractors, Repositories, and Figures    [Go to top]

We use the following feature extractors:
CREPE (vocal pitch)
wav2vec2.0 fine-tuned on the TIMIT dataset (PPG)
Multi-instrument Onsets and Frames (piano roll)

For isolating the vocals from data mixed with instrumental accompaniment, we use the Demucs source separation model.

The compared acoustic models are derived from the following repositories:
Matcha-TTS
ForwardTacotron
T5 DDPM
